# Speaker Guidance

The slides are not the presentation. The speaker is.

Slides are a visual aid for the audience. Speaker notes are a preparation tool for the speaker. The two serve different audiences and should be designed separately.

---

## Speaker Notes Philosophy

Speaker notes are not a script. They are a map.

A script creates a speaker who reads. A map creates a speaker who navigates. Write notes that trigger your thinking rather than replace it.

**Include in speaker notes:**
- The key point this slide makes in your own words (not a repeat of the headline)
- The transition into this slide ("given what we just established about X, this is why Y matters")
- The transition out ("so given that, what does it mean for...")
- Any likely questions this slide will trigger
- Timing target for this slide

**Do not include:**
- Word-for-word scripts
- Repetition of what is already visible on the slide
- Placeholder notes ("talk about this more")

Speaker notes must be complete enough to function as a standalone document — a reader who cannot see the slides should be able to follow the full presentation through notes alone.

---

## Pacing

A well-paced presentation breathes. It gives the audience time to process before moving on while maintaining enough momentum to hold attention.

**General timing guidelines:**

| Slide Type | Target Time |
|---|---|
| Title / opening | 60–90 seconds |
| Content slide with data | 90–120 seconds |
| Concept slide, single key idea | 60–90 seconds |
| Transition / section break | 20–30 seconds |
| Q&A setup / closing | 60 seconds |

**Pacing killers:**
- Reading bullet points aloud
- Apologizing for a slide ("sorry, this is a busy one")
- Rushing through the end of a section to recover lost time

If running over time: cut from the end, not the middle. The middle is the argument; the end is the wrap-up. The closing is the least critical section to compress.

---

## Transitions

Verbal transitions between slides and sections are as important as the slides themselves. They are what transforms a sequence of slides into a unified story.

**Types of verbal transitions:**

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Logical connector | "And this is exactly why..." |
| Consequence | "So what does that mean for us?" |
| Contrast | "But here's what we didn't expect." |
| Escalation | "And it gets more interesting." |
| Time | "Fast forward to six months later." |
| Pivot | "That's the problem. Let me show you the solution." |

Write at least one explicit transition in the speaker notes for every slide. Transitions are the connective tissue of the story — they are not optional.

---

## Discussion Prompts

For presentations that include Q&A or interactive discussion, prepare discussion prompts in advance.

**Types of prompts:**

- **Warm-up:** "Before I show you what we found, what would you expect?"
- **Check-in:** "Does this match what you're seeing in your area?"
- **Challenge:** "What's the strongest objection to this approach?"
- **Commitment:** "What would need to be true for you to support this?"

Place discussion prompts in speaker notes at the slides where they are most relevant. Do not improvise prompts during delivery — they are almost always weaker than prepared ones.

## Audience Engagement

**Eye contact:** Present to individuals, not to the room. Move eye contact naturally — hold for 2–3 seconds per person before moving. Never sweep the room mechanically or stare at the screen.

**Silence:** Silence is not empty space. It is processing time. After making a key point, pause for 2–3 seconds before moving on. The audience needs a moment to absorb before you continue. Silence signals confidence.

**Questions during the presentation:** Decide in advance whether you will take questions during or after. State your preference at the start. If taking questions during: stay in control of time; offer to "put a pin in" deep questions for the end. Never let a question derail the narrative.

---

## Timing

Rehearse with a timer. The first rehearsal is always 20–30% longer than expected.

**Time allocation guidance:**

| Total Length | Content | Q&A / Buffer |
|---|---|---|
| 15 minutes | 12 minutes | 3 minutes |
| 30 minutes | 24 minutes | 6 minutes |
| 60 minutes | 45 minutes | 15 minutes |
| Workshop (90+ min) | 50% instruction | 50% activity |

Never go over on instruction time in a workshop. Participants came to do, not just listen.

---

## The Apology Slide

An apology slide is any slide the speaker expects to apologize for — "sorry, this is a busy slide" or "I know this is hard to read."

**If you are planning to apologize for a slide, fix the slide instead.**

The need to apologize is always a signal that the slide is doing too much, the font is too small, or the design needs to be simplified. Address the root cause; do not apologize for it.
