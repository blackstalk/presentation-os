# Storytelling

Great presentations are not information delivery systems. They are guided experiences that change how an audience thinks.

## The Three-Layer Model

Every slide, every section, and every complete presentation operates on three layers:

**Layer 1 — What**
The information being communicated. Data, facts, findings, status.

**Layer 2 — Why**
Why this information matters to this specific audience, right now. Relevance is earned, not assumed.

**Layer 3 — So What**
What the audience should think, feel, or do differently as a result. The call to action, belief shift, or decision this slide exists to produce.

A slide that delivers only Layer 1 is a document.
A slide that delivers Layer 1 and Layer 2 is a briefing.
A slide that delivers all three is a presentation.

## Narrative Architecture

### Opening

Open with **tension**, not context.

Context is background. Tension is a question the audience needs answered, a problem they recognize, or a gap between where they are and where they want to be.

- **Weak opening:** "Today we'll cover three topics: background, findings, and next steps."
- **Strong opening:** "We have a problem. Our most engaged users are churning at twice the rate of less engaged ones. Here's what we found."

The first 90 seconds determine whether the audience is with you or managing their inbox. Earn their attention before asking for it.

### Body

Build the story in **sections**, each of which earns its place by moving the argument forward.

Each section should:
- Open with a question or tension specific to that section
- Deliver evidence or reasoning that resolves it
- Land on an insight that advances the overall argument

If a section cannot answer "why does this section exist in this presentation for this audience," cut it.

### Closing

End with **clarity**, not summary.

A summary restates what was said. A strong close answers: "Given everything we've covered — what now?"

The final slide should leave the audience with a clear next step, decision point, or belief shift. Not a thank-you slide. Not a Q&A placeholder. A conclusion.

## Headline Writing

The slide headline is the most important line of text on the slide.

**Arguments, not labels:**

| Label (weak) | Argument (strong) |
|---|---|
| "Q3 Results" | "Q3 revenue grew 18% — driven entirely by mid-market expansion" |
| "System Architecture" | "The new architecture eliminates the single point of failure" |
| "Next Steps" | "Three decisions needed by Friday to keep the timeline intact" |

The headline should be true without the supporting content. The supporting content should make the headline undeniable.

**One headline per slide, always.** If a slide has two headlines, it is two slides.

## Pacing

Pacing is the management of audience attention over time.

- **Slow down** for complex ideas — give the audience time to process before adding more
- **Speed up** for transitions — move briskly between sections so momentum builds
- **Pause before reveals** — silence is a pacing tool
- **Use fragments** to control what the audience sees and when — never show the answer before asking the question

A 30-minute presentation with 60 slides is almost certainly moving too fast. A 30-minute presentation with 12 slides may be moving too slowly. Pacing is judged by comprehension, not slide count.

## Audience Adaptation

The same information should be presented differently to different audiences.

| Audience | Priority | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Executive | Decisions, implications, risk | Confident, direct |
| Technical | Implementation, tradeoffs, precision | Rigorous, detailed |
| Customer | Outcomes, value, ease | Warm, benefit-focused |
| Internal | Context, alignment, next steps | Collaborative, honest |

Audience adaptation is not about dumbing down. It is about relevance. What is "too much detail" for one audience is "not enough rigor" for another.

Always identify the primary audience before any slide is written. The secondary audience is secondary — design for the primary, accommodate the secondary where you can without compromising the former.

---

## The Cut Test

Before finalizing any slide, apply the cut test:

1. If this slide were removed, would the audience's understanding of the argument change?
2. If the answer is no — cut the slide.
3. If the answer is "slightly" — merge it with an adjacent slide.
4. If the answer is "yes, meaningfully" — keep it.

Most first drafts fail this test on 20–30% of slides.
